01 — FOUNDATIONS

WHAT IS NLP
AND WHY IT
MATTERS

DEFINITION

Natural Language Processing (NLP) combines AI, Computer Science, and Linguistics to make machines understand and generate human language.

Works with English, Hindi, French, Tamil, Arabic, and more.
Human language is ambiguous, context-dependent, and constantly evolving.
MAIN GOALS
Scientific Goal: understand how language represents meaning.
Engineering Goal: build usable language-processing systems.
TRANSLATION SEARCH CHATBOTS SUMMARIZATION
WHY IMPORTANT

Most human knowledge is in text: news, books, emails, legal docs, web pages, research papers, social posts.

CORE NEED

Data volume is too high for manual reading. NLP automates extraction and interpretation at scale.

OUTCOME

Faster information access, multilingual systems, and practical automation in communication workflows.

02 — LANGUAGE COMPLEXITY

WHY NLP IS
HARD: AMBIGUITY

LEXICAL AMBIGUITY

One word can have multiple meanings depending on context.

Example idea: "Will Will will Will's will?" where "Will" appears as name, auxiliary, and noun.

STRUCTURAL AMBIGUITY

A sentence can have multiple parse trees.

"The man saw the boy with the binoculars."
Did the man use binoculars, or did the boy have them?

SEMANTIC AMBIGUITY

Same words, different interpretations.

"Flying planes can be dangerous."
It may mean piloting planes is dangerous, or planes that are flying are dangerous.

VAGUENESS

Natural language often lacks precise boundaries.

Phrases like "It is warm" or "She must have" require context to determine exact meaning.

PARSING EXPLOSION

As sentence length increases, possible parse structures grow rapidly. That combinatorial growth makes language understanding computationally expensive.

03 — ADDITIONAL CHALLENGES

FROM SOCIAL
TEXT TO IDIOMS

NON-STANDARD TEXT

Examples: "u", "gr8", "sooo happyyy". Requires robust normalization and preprocessing.

SEGMENTATION

Token boundaries are not always obvious, especially in hashtags, compounds, and scripts without whitespace.

IDIOMS

Phrases like "burn the midnight oil" are non-literal. Word-by-word interpretation fails.

NEOLOGISMS

Vocabulary evolves: "unfriend", "retweet", and verbified brand names enter language rapidly.

SENSE SHIFT

Existing words acquire new meanings (e.g., "sick" meaning "great"). Context becomes essential.

ENTITY AMBIGUITY

A phrase can be a person, title, place, or product depending on context.

NATURAL LANGUAGE

Ambiguous, flexible, informal, context-driven.

PROGRAMMING LANGUAGE

Strict syntax, deterministic interpretation, machine-precise semantics.

04 — CORPUS BASICS

TYPE, TOKEN,
TTR & WORD
FREQUENCY

TYPE VS TOKEN

Sentence: cat cat dog
Tokens = 3 (all occurrences)
Types = 2 (unique words: cat, dog)

TYPE-TOKEN RATIO

TTR = Types / Tokens. Higher TTR suggests richer vocabulary, but TTR depends strongly on text length.

Tom Sawyer: 8018/71370 = 0.112
Shakespeare: 29066/884647 = 0.032
FUNCTION WORDS

Grammatical glue with low standalone meaning: the, a, and, of, to.

CONTENT WORDS

Carry semantic meaning: computer, teacher, democracy, happiness.

HAPAX LEGOMENA

Words appearing exactly once in a corpus. Important for sparsity and language modeling.

05 — EMPIRICAL LAW

ZIPF'S LAW
AND THE LONG TAIL

CORE IDEA

Frequency is inversely proportional to rank:
f ∝ 1 / r
or approximately f × r = constant

Few words are extremely common. Most words are rare.

NLP IMPACT

Advantages: common words are easy to model, stopword filtering helps efficiency.

Challenges: rare words have limited training data, causing sparsity issues.
